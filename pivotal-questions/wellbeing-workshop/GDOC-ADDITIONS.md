## Tab 2: Benjamin et al.

**Add under "Key Findings" or as new section "Benjamin's Broader Research Program":**

```
Benjamin's Broader Research Program

Beyond scale-use heterogeneity, Benjamin's research questions reliance on single broad measures. Key insight from correspondence:

"People's choices suggest that they care about more than just life satisfaction and happiness."

Proposed "Ideal Approach" (Benjamin):
1. Collect comprehensive wellbeing data across multiple specific dimensions
2. Use stated-preference surveys to estimate how people trade off wellbeing aspects
3. Create individual-level "personal wellbeing indices" for policy evaluation
4. Apply calibration questions for interpersonal comparability

Practical Shortcut:
• Importance ratings correlate 0.8-0.9 with stated-preference tradeoffs
• Visual calibrations offer partial scale-use corrections when full surveys too costly

Implementation Context:
• Israeli think tank (Reingewertz) implementing these methods—funding dependent
• Benjamin suggested inviting Yaniv Reingewertz and Dimitry Taubinsky to discuss empirical applications
```

## Tab 3: Evaluator Discussion

**Add under "Caspar Kaiser" as context:**

```
Caspar Kaiser's Four Key Concerns (from pre-workshop discussion)

1. Comparability: Can we trust 0-10 life satisfaction to mean the same across populations?

2. Linearity: Does a move from 3→4 mean the same as 7→8 on the scale?

3. Neutral Point: Where is the "neutral point" on the satisfaction scale? Is it 5? 0? Something else?

4. Right Concepts: Are we measuring the right concepts? Life satisfaction vs. happiness vs. flourishing?

Central Tension for Discussion:
• Can we trust intervention effects on stated well-being given potential experimenter demand effects?
• How do we distinguish genuine welfare improvements from reporting artifacts?

## Tab 5: DALY-WELLBY

**Add under "Current Approaches" or as new section:**

```
Conversion Factor Range from Literature

Current estimates span a wide range: 2-15 WELLBYs per DALY across studies.

This variation may be driven by:
• Different populations and contexts
• Starting baseline (median vs. lower percentiles)
• Domain (mental health vs. physical health vs. consumption)
• Methodological differences (stated preference vs. revealed preference)

Key Methodological Concern (from Lerner):
Linearizing the DALY-WELLBY conversion may be inappropriate. The relationship may vary non-linearly depending on:
• Severity of baseline condition
• Duration of impact
• Age and life stage

Discussion Prompts from Pre-Workshop Planning

1. What conversion approach minimizes expected welfare loss in funding decisions?

2. Should the conversion factor vary by intervention domain?

3. When funders get dramatically different rankings from WELLBY vs. DALY methods, how should they communicate this?

4. What data collection would most reduce uncertainty around the conversion factor?

5. Is a single conversion factor even the right framing, or should we think about ranges/distributions?
