Section One: The Decision Problem

Organizations comparing interventions—especially in low- and middle-income countries—face a measurement problem. Interventions change different things: mortality, morbidity, consumption, mental health, social cohesion. The WELLBY approach proposes translating these into a common unit based on subjective wellbeing, enabling "welfare impact per dollar" comparisons.

The original proposal for WELLBYs came from Frijters, Clark, Krekel, and Layard in 2020, in a paper titled "A Happy Choice: Wellbeing as the Goal of Government," published in Health Economics.

A focal question for this workshop is: How reliably can we compare interventions by aggregating changes in reported wellbeing—the WELLBY approach—especially across different studies and contexts?

This is one of several comparison frameworks. Others include DALY and QALY-based approaches, capability approaches, and direct monetary valuation. The workshop examines the linear WELLBY's reliability relative to these alternatives, not in isolation.

Let me describe the measurement-to-decision pipeline. Imagine a flow chart with five stages:

First, the intervention—the program being evaluated.

Second, the study design—how it's researched, whether through a randomized controlled trial, quasi-experiment, or observational study.

Third, measured outcomes—what the study actually records. Some use life satisfaction, others use DALYs or depression scales.

Fourth, the translation layer—where different metrics must be mapped or calibrated to enable comparison.

Fifth, the common currency—a single unit, whether WELLBYs, DALYs, or dollars, enabling apples-to-apples comparison for ranking and allocation decisions.

Each arrow in this pipeline involves assumptions that can introduce error or bias. The workshop focuses on where these assumptions are most likely to matter.

The workshop has three goals. First, clarity about which assumptions matter most for which comparisons, and what evidence would change views. Second, sharing information and synthesizing participant expertise. Third, generating practical insights and actionable recommendations for funders working with current evidence.

Section Two: Definitions and Key Concepts

One WELLBY equals a one-point change in life satisfaction on a zero-to-ten scale, for one person, sustained for one year. This definition comes from the UK Green Book Wellbeing Guidance published by HM Treasury.

Let me explain the origins and adoption of this concept.

The original proposal came from Frijters and colleagues in 2020. Most usage defines WELLBY using life satisfaction—measured by the Cantril ladder on a zero-to-ten scale—but some researchers use affect-based measures like experienced happiness. The choice matters: life satisfaction captures evaluative wellbeing, while affect captures momentary experience.

Several organizations have adopted WELLBYs. The Happier Lives Institute, led by Michael Plant and colleagues, uses WELLBYs as their primary metric for charity comparison and has produced extensive cost-effectiveness analyses. Founders Pledge uses WELLBYs alongside DALYs for mental health cost-effectiveness analysis. GiveWell has explored WELLBY analysis but with significant reservations. And the UK Government uses WELLBYs in official guidance for policy appraisal.

The standard life satisfaction questions take two forms.

The OECD single-item question asks: "Overall, how satisfied are you with your life as a whole these days?" Respondents answer on a scale from zero—meaning "not at all satisfied"—to ten—meaning "completely satisfied."

The Cantril ladder, used by Gallup, asks: "Please imagine a ladder, with steps numbered from zero at the bottom to ten at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you, and the bottom represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?"

These two framings—satisfaction versus ladder position—are often used interchangeably, but may capture subtly different constructs. Cross-study comparisons should note which instrument was used.

There's an important distinction between incremental and level-based WELLBY accounting.

Incremental WELLBYs—which is what most intervention comparisons need—measure the change in life satisfaction relative to a counterfactual. Mathematically, this is the sum across individuals and time periods of the life satisfaction under intervention minus the life satisfaction under the control condition.

Level-based WELLBYs are used for mortality comparisons. Here, you're summing the absolute level of life satisfaction across life-years. This becomes important when comparing programs that extend life versus programs that improve quality of life for the living.

Section Three: Core Assumptions

Using linear WELLBYs for cross-intervention comparison requires assumptions. A common overstatement is that "equal scores mean equal welfare"—but this is stronger than most applications need.

There are four core assumptions.

First, cardinality, also called linearity. This assumes that equal intervals on the scale imply equal welfare differences. Moving from three to four on the scale represents the same welfare gain as moving from seven to eight. If this is violated, summing may distort comparisons. Michael Plant's 2025 working paper explores conditions under which treating life satisfaction as cardinal is defensible.

Second, unit-change comparability. A one-point change has approximately the same welfare meaning across people. This is weaker than requiring equal levels to mean equal welfare.

Third, temporal aggregation. This assumes that integrating wellbeing over time is meaningful. This may fail if adaptation returns people to baseline, or if respondents reinterpret the scale over time—a phenomenon called response shift.

Fourth, cross-domain capture. This assumes that life satisfaction incorporates welfare from many domains—health, income, relationships—not merely transient mood.

Let me discuss the time structure and discounting.

Most studies measure outcomes at baseline and one or two follow-ups. For example, Haushofer and Shapiro measured subjective wellbeing at nine months and three years after cash transfers in Kenya. StrongMinds studies typically follow up at three to six months post-treatment.

Extrapolating beyond measured timepoints involves uncertainty about persistence—does the effect last?—and discounting—are future wellbeing gains worth less than present ones?

There's a distinct threat called response shift. Even if baseline scale-use heterogeneity cancels through randomization, treatment can change the meaning of the respondent's self-evaluation. Response shift refers to changes in internal standards, values, or conceptualization that alter how respondents answer over time.

For wellbeing interventions—especially psychosocial programs that may explicitly reframe cognition—response shift is not a rare edge case. If treatment changes how people use the scale, the observed change in life satisfaction mixes "true welfare change" with "scale change," potentially biasing the WELLBY estimate in either direction.

Section Four: A Key Critique—Identification and Transformations

Bond and Lang, in a 2019 paper in the Journal of Political Economy titled "The Sad Truth about Happiness Scales," argue that with ordinal happiness data, comparing "average happiness" between groups is not identified without strong assumptions. Monotonic transformations can reverse conclusions.

What does "non-identified" mean? A parameter is identified when data plus assumptions pin down a unique value. Ordinal responses only tell us which interval a latent value falls into. Many different latent distributions and transformations can generate the same observed category counts, so rankings of means can change across equally admissible representations.

Why does this matter for intervention comparison?

For cross-study synthesis, comparing treatment effects measured on different scales can inherit the same vulnerability.

For magnitude-sensitive cost-effectiveness, even if signs are stable, cost-effectiveness ratios rely on magnitudes.

Let me describe a thought experiment about transformation sensitivity. Imagine you have two interventions. Intervention A produces a small effect—one point—but at high baseline life satisfaction levels. Intervention B produces a larger effect—two points—but at low baseline levels.

Under the linear assumption, B looks better. But if the true welfare function is convex—meaning gains at higher levels are more valuable—then A might actually be better. Move the transformation parameter and you can flip which intervention appears superior.

This is the core of the Bond and Lang critique: without knowing the true shape of the welfare function, ranking interventions by average life satisfaction changes is not robust.

Section Five: Scale-Use Heterogeneity—Shifters versus Stretchers

A useful decomposition is the affine model, which separates two types of scale-use differences across respondents.

Mathematically, latent welfare equals some intercept term plus a slope term multiplied by reported life satisfaction.

"Shifters" have different intercepts—some people always report two points higher than their welfare would indicate. With shifters, levels are not comparable across people, but differences are. The change in welfare equals the slope times the change in life satisfaction, and if everyone has the same slope, this works.

"Stretchers" have different slopes—some people compress the scale. With stretchers, both levels and differences fail to be comparable. The change in welfare for person i equals their individual slope times their change in life satisfaction. This can reverse cross-population comparisons.

Benjamin and colleagues—in a 2023 NBER working paper titled "Adjusting for Scale-Use Heterogeneity"—propose calibration questions to identify and adjust for scale-use heterogeneity. These are questions designed to have the same objective answer across respondents, allowing researchers to estimate each person's reporting function.

Let me illustrate with an example. Suppose Population A shows a reported change of 0.5 points in life satisfaction, but their stretch factor is 0.8—they compress the scale. Population B shows the same 0.5 point change, but their stretch factor is 1.2—they spread out the scale.

The naive comparison says they're equal. But the welfare-adjusted comparison gives Population A an implied welfare change of 0.4 and Population B a change of 0.6. The ranking reverses when you account for different scale use.

A common question is: why don't fixed effects solve this? Fixed effects absorb level differences—the intercept terms. But if people have different stretch factors, the implied welfare change per reported point differs, and fixed effects don't address this.

Section Six: The Neutral Point and Mortality

Two different "zeros" come up in WELLBY discussions.

First, neutral wellbeing—the point where life is neither good nor bad.

Second, death as zero—treating dead people as scoring zero for life-year calculations.

For incremental comparisons among the living, the neutral point often cancels out. When comparing the change in life satisfaction between intervention and control groups, both are measured from the same implicit baseline. The zero point subtracts from both sides and doesn't affect the difference.

But for mortality comparisons—where WELLBYs from life extension equals life-years times average wellbeing—the origin is load-bearing.

Let me illustrate with an example. Suppose a mortality intervention prevents a death, yielding forty additional life-years at an average life satisfaction of five.

If the neutral point is zero, the benefit equals forty times five, or 200 WELLBYs.

If the neutral point is two—meaning life satisfaction below two is experienced as negative—then the benefit equals forty times the quantity five minus two, which is 120 "above-neutral" WELLBYs.

This forty percent difference is ranking-relevant when comparing mortality-focused interventions to non-mortality wellbeing programs.

The key takeaway: When does the neutral point matter? When comparing mortality-focused interventions to non-mortality wellbeing programs. For comparisons among living people only, it typically cancels because differences are unaffected by the choice of zero point.

Section Seven: Evidence and Alternatives

On reliability: single-item life evaluations have test-retest correlations around 0.5 to 0.7 over short windows. This comes from Krueger and Schkade's 2008 paper "The reliability of subjective well-being measures." This means measurement error attenuates estimated effects—small real effects may be undervalued. Life satisfaction measures are noisy but not useless.

On predictive validity: Kaiser and Oswald, in a 2022 paper in PNAS titled "The Scientific Value of Numerical Measures of Human Feelings," show that single numeric feelings responses predict consequential outcomes—changing neighborhoods, jobs, and partners. The relationships tend to be replicable and close to linear.

Let me discuss evidence from low- and middle-income countries, specifically from GiveDirectly cash transfer trials in Kenya.

Haushofer and Shapiro studied unconditional cash transfers. In the short run—around nine months—they found life satisfaction increased by 0.17 standard deviations and happiness by 0.16 standard deviations, using World Values Survey measures. In the long run—around three years—life satisfaction still showed a statistically significant increase of 0.08 standard deviations, and the psychological wellbeing index increased by 0.16 standard deviations.

These findings show that life satisfaction measures have detectable signal in LMIC randomized trials—not just noise—and effects can persist.

However, there's a measurement layer problem. Many LMIC mental health studies report depression scales or symptom indices, not standard zero-to-ten life satisfaction. GiveWell's assessment of the Happier Lives Institute's StrongMinds analysis explicitly highlights uncertainty in translating depression improvements into life satisfaction gains.

Even if you accept WELLBY as the target unit, the measurement layer forces choices. You could use DALYs and QALYs, which are more standard in health evaluation, even if they miss non-health welfare. You could use life satisfaction directly, but only where trials collect it. Or you could use mapping models from depression to life satisfaction, but carry the mapping uncertainty explicitly.

Let me compare WELLBYs with alternatives.

WELLBYs have strengths: they capture non-health welfare, use direct self-report, and have low respondent burden. Their weaknesses include scale-use and comparability assumptions, and cross-study synthesis issues.

DALYs and QALYs have strengths: they're standardized, have large evidence bases, and have direct mortality links. Their weaknesses include potentially missing non-health welfare, and mental health disability weights are contentious.

Calibrated WELLBYs—using the Benjamin et al. methods—can reduce scale-use bias by thirty to fifty percent. But they're complex, their feasibility in LMIC settings is unclear, and they introduce new assumptions.

Section Eight: The WELLBY Calculator

I'll describe how to estimate incremental WELLBYs.

Multiply four quantities: effect size in life satisfaction points, duration in years, number of recipients, and then divide by cost to get cost-effectiveness.

For example, if an intervention produces a 0.5 point increase in life satisfaction, lasting two years, for 1000 recipients, the total WELLBYs equal 0.5 times 2 times 1000, which is 1000 WELLBYs. If the program cost 100,000 dollars, the cost per WELLBY is 100 dollars.

This assumes constant effect size. Real applications should account for effect decay, discounting, and uncertainty.

Section Nine: Practical Considerations

For funders comparing interventions:

Treat WELLBY estimates as one input among several, not the final answer.

Conduct sensitivity analyses across different neutral points and scale-comparability assumptions.

Report how rankings change under different assumptions.

Be explicit about uncertainty rather than using false precision.

For researchers designing studies:

Use standardized life satisfaction questions—the OECD prototype—to enable cross-study comparison.

Include long-term follow-up to capture duration effects and potential adaptation.

Consider calibration questions or vignettes if comparing across populations.

Pre-register analysis plans including functional form choices.

Conditions that support stronger inference include: large effect sizes that overwhelm measurement error, within-person designs where each person serves as their own control, multiple wellbeing measures showing the same directional effect, and triangulation with behavioral or objective outcomes.

Section Ten: Open Questions—A Research Agenda

Here are high-value areas for future research that could meaningfully improve the reliability of WELLBY-based comparisons.

First, neutral point estimation. What is the actual neutral point on the zero-to-ten scale for different populations? How stable is it across contexts?

Second, scale-use heterogeneity mapping. How do shifters versus stretchers vary across LMIC populations? Can we predict which matters more?

Third, cheap calibration methods. Can vignettes, anchoring questions, or other calibration approaches work in low-resource settings without excessive burden?

Fourth, the WELLBY-DALY relationship. What's the mapping between WELLBYs and DALYs? Is it linear? How much does it vary by health condition?

Fifth, demand effects and response shift. How do experimenter demand effects and response shift vary by intervention type?
